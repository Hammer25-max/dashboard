Where It Started

I Was Six Years Old.

My father passed away when I was six years old. Alcohol took him. And what came after that shaped everything — the good and the hard. My mother did everything she could but there were days with no electricity because she couldn't pay the bill. Days with no running water. Hard days. Days where seeing a brighter future felt impossible because nothing around me was pointing toward one.

I couldn't fathom having my own place someday. Couldn't picture getting on a plane. Couldn't imagine any version of the life I have now. The lens I was looking through was cracked and everything coming through it looked broken. It was hard to fathom even where I am at today.

"Nobody saw those days. Nobody saw the repercussions of losing a father at a young age. Nobody saw what that does to a mother, to a household, to a kid trying to figure out what the future even looks like when it feels like there isn't one."

The statistics were not on my side. And they are not on the side of most kids in that situation.

Not because I was special. Not because I had something figured out. Because somewhere along the way I found the one thing I could control — and I held onto it.

The Invisible Work

The Gym At Fifteen.
No Plan.
Just Showed Up.

At fifteen years old my mother signed me up at the local Y for a program called Athletes of Tomorrow. Three days a week in the gym. I didn't fully understand what I was walking into. I didn't know at the time that the repetition, the showing up, the structure — all of it was laying groundwork for something I couldn't see yet.

Here's what I know now: values inform decisions that have not yet been made yet. The values being built in that gym three days a week were quietly programming the choices I would make years later — in situations I couldn't have anticipated, under pressure I hadn't faced yet. I had no idea. I just kept going back.

What nobody saw were the years that followed. And here is the thing about the values being built in that gym — discipline, consistency, accountability, work ethic — I wasn't consciously applying them. They were just in me. Baked in. When the workload came — three jobs, full college schedule, the gym — I didn't question it. I didn't look at it and decide whether I could handle it. I just handled it. Because the gym had already built the framework. It opened the door to having no excuses. It didn't matter what the load was. I could take it on. And through taking it on, something became undeniable.

Not A Body.
A Foundation.

Each rep pushing past failure when you don't want to go. When you have nothing left and every reasonable voice says stop. You keep going anyway. Not for anyone watching. Because you decided you were going to. That is where the real work happens — not in the comfortable sets, in the ones where you had every reason to quit and didn't.

The gym calloused the mind. And a calloused mind transfers. The four values built under that bar showed up in every area of life — in ways I didn't plan for and couldn't have predicted.

The Counter Argument

"But You Started Young."

I hear this. And I understand it. If you are 35 and just starting, it can feel like you missed a window. Like someone got a head start on a race you didn't know you were running.

Here's what's true: the brain is more malleable before 25. Habits built young compound longer. The frontal lobe isn't fully developed until around 25 — and that means starting younger comes with a certain plasticity that makes building habits feel more natural. Yes. That part is real.

Here's What's Also True

At 35 your prefrontal cortex is fully developed. That's the part of your brain responsible for planning, decision-making, and impulse control. A teenager goes to the gym because someone took them or dragged them there. A 35-year-old goes because they consciously chose to.

That choice — made with full awareness of the cost, the difficulty, the sacrifice required — is actually more powerful than anything I did at 15 when I had no idea what I was building or why. You are making a deliberate decision with a fully developed brain. That's not a disadvantage. That's a different kind of strength.

The discipline built after 25 is harder won. And harder won means worth more. Every year you wait is a choice. Every year you don't is a choice too. The question is which choice you can live with.

"The decision not to try is still a decision. At any age. Every day you don't start is a choice. Every day you do — that is a choice too. Your brain is fully equipped to make it. So is your life."
What Gets Built Over Time

A Quiet Humble
Confidence.

People have told me they see it. I don't think about it much. That's kind of the whole point.

The less you can be offended in life the better. Most things don't get to me. Not because I'm cold or detached but because I know who I am, what I am about, and what I am capable of. That knowing — that bedrock certainty — came from one place. Showing up for myself every single day in private when no one was watching and no one was holding me accountable.

You cannot fake that kind of confidence. It is not affirmations or positive thinking. It is the accumulated evidence of a thousand kept promises to yourself. The alarm that went off and you got up. The session that happened when you didn't feel like it. The meal that got prepped when ordering out was easier. The day you came back after falling off instead of using the fall as a reason to stop.

"I'm not perfect. There were mistakes along the way and there always will be. Nobody can be perfect. But for the most part I have lived a clean life — not because I'm better than anyone — but because having no discipline and no values costs you. I had seen that cost up close. I knew what it looked like. I decided early that I wasn't paying it."

When I fell off I got right back to it. Not with a dramatic restart. Not waiting for Monday. Just back to it. The next day. And by practicing that return — over and over — I built something undeniable. A mind that is battle tested. Ready for what life is going to throw at it. And life has thrown plenty.

I built something quietly over years and I am reaping the benefits of it now. It compounded into family, relationships, work, decisions. Into how I handle hard moments. Into how I show up every single day. The grind that nobody saw became the foundation for everything they do see.
